Hard Disks

  • Basic interface: Sequence of sectors (typically 512 bytes).
  • A stack of spinning platters.
    • Typically 7,200 to 15,000 RPM.
    • 10000 RPM is 6ms per rotation.
  • Each platter is a set of concentric circles called tracks.
  • read/write head is on the end of an arm

Components of latency

  • Need to position the head over the right track (called seek)
    • Speed up
    • coast
    • slow down
    • settle (0.5 to 2ms)
  • Rotational latency (wait for platter to come underneath)
    • Average of 3ms on 10,000 RPM disk
  • Transfer

Interesting geometry

  • Logical sectors in neighboring tracks are offset.
    • Why?
    • Head can’t move instantly. Want the next sector to be under the head when it settles on the text track.
    • See Figure 37.4
  • Outer tracks are longer than inner tracks
    • What are the consequences of this?
    • Either there are more sectors in the outer tracks, or
    • Sectors are less dense in the outer tracks.
    • At the same density, bits go by faster.

Performance

  • Transfer time is effectively nothing compared to the time to physically move the disk.
  • Disks with transfer rates of over 100MB/s have effective I/O rates of random workloads of < 1 MBs.
  • For a completely sequential workload, the I/O rates jump to close to the transfer rate.
  • Thus data layout is critical to performance.

Disk Scheduling

  • Suppose multiple requests come in while the disk is serving a previous request. When the current request is complete, what order should the queued requests be served in? Remember: Minimizing seek time and rotational latency has a huge effect on performance.
  • SSTF: Shortest Seek Time First
    • Challenge: OS does not have access to specific disk geometry
    • Can approximate with Nearest Block First
    • Starvation is possible
  • Elevator (aka SCAN)
    • Just move head back and forth from outside to inside.
  • C-SCAN (Circular scan)
    • Only go outside to inside
    • makes seek times more consistent
    • Can be important in real-time systems, or other systems where predictability is important.
  • At first, OS did all the scheduling.
  • If we let disks do the scheduling (i.e., accept multiple requests), they can optimize better.
  • Hybrid: Disk has fix number of concurrent requests. OS needs to “guess” at best x requests to send in parallel.
  • Overall efficiency can sometimes be improved by not issuing requests immediately.
    • Think of a partially full taxi waiting a few minutes before leaving the airport in case another customer comes along with a nearby destination.
